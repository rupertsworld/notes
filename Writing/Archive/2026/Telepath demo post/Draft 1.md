# Imagining a new kind of personal computer

Throughout my career I've existed in very demo-heavy cultures. At Google Creative Lab, demos would spin off into public-facing projects we shipped to millions of users. At Adept, I remember a time our design team catalyzed a tectonic shift in company direction with a one-minute demo at our all-hands on a Friday afternoon.

I've seen the power of the collective imagination that a good demo unlocks, and it's an awesome catalyst for invention and progress.

And yet, when I look around at the state of the AI industry, while I see lots of cool products springing up everywhere, I haven't found a single visionary demo that imagines how this will change computing more broadly, post-ChatGPT.

So, a few months ago, we at [Telepath](https://telepath.computer) set out to demonstrate what an AI-first personal computer should look like, according to *us*.

## Background on the demo

Ok, so let's unpack this a little.

The video above was produced from our internal designs in partnership with Territory, a studio known for producing screen graphics for films you've definitely heard of like *Blade Runner 2049*, *Dune*, *Ad Astra* and *Prometheus*. We were inspired in part by the film *Her*, but not for its huskily-voiced AI, but rather the humanistic, depth-based and textural screens (thank you ... for a great talk about this at Betaworks).

In our discovery process we drew from our initial more focused product research, but also conducted user interviews to understand where people struggled with computers more broadly. In addition, we brought reams of sketches and thinking that we'd conducted previously and an analysis of capabilities we felt were on the horizon. A vision demo is an inexact science, and an act of creative exploration and alignment as opposed to pure problem solving, though it shouldn't exist unmoored from reality.

There are three core capabilities that formed the basis of the story we wanted to tell: generative interfaces, sensemaking, and situational awareness.

Let's start with **dynamic interfaces.** In a traditional personal computer, humans need to do all the work. Every piece of instruction must be created by a human being and given to the computer. Writing code is hard and complicated, so software developers go off and create applications that roughy solve a problem for an average group of users.

In a computer built around LLMs, applications are no longer needed. This computer is able to synthesize its own code, to produce software that achieves a user's goals. This solves all sorts of problems: did you ever have a to-do app that you loved, but got annoyed when it went through a redesign? Do you need to tackle a specific business process in your organization, and no apps really cut it so you resort to a janky spreadsheet? Are you a bit pissed off by *Liquid Glass*? Congratulations, generative software makes this all go away.

But creating and distributing generative software on the fly also unlocks some brand new opportunities that we are yet to really grasp the impact of.

You'll notice that in our demo there are no apps. Rather, the user interface fluidly reacts to display cards that represent objects relevant to the current conversation, like an email, note, or a higher-level concept or summary. These cards can in turn morph or semantically zoom to display different aspects of that underlying object, such as specific pull quotes to support an argument.
