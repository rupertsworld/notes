# What Is The Web? - Draft

Is the web HTML and CSS and JavaScript? Is it browsers and HTTP?

I'd argue the web is really about open networked computing. It's an approach to technology: openness, interoperability, open standards. The ability to view source. The ability to link. The idea that no one company controls the whole stack.

And we need to bring those same principles to the AI era.

Because right now, the dominant AI platforms are proprietary walled gardens. We're in the 1980s with CompuServe and AOL all over again.

But here's the thing: the open web came *after* those walled gardens, and it still won. CompuServe launched in 1979. AOL in 1985. Tim Berners-Lee didn't even propose the web until 1989, and it didn't go public until 1991. The walled gardens had a decade head start.

And yet the open web won. Because openness is more powerful. Because interoperability creates more value. Because when you don't lock people in, innovation flourishes.

We have an opportunity right now. We're at ground level for building the next open computing paradigm. We did it once before, at the dawn of the web. We can do it again.

This time, we don't have to wait ten years for the walled gardens to dominate before building the open alternative. We can build it now. We can make openness the default, not the insurgent.

But it requires us - humans who understand and care about these principles - to advocate for them and build them into this next era.

# Information Distribution - Draft

But software is only half the story. The other half is information.

Because here's the thing: you can synthesize software, but you can't synthesize information. If I want to know what's happening in the world, or what my friends are up to, or what the best restaurants in Melbourne are, that information has to come from somewhere. Real humans, real experiences, real knowledge. 

In the demo I showed you, the computer was pulling information from the network and making sense of it for the user. That's actually closer to the original vision of the web than what we have today. Tim Berners-Lee talked about "user agents" – software that would act on your behalf, mediating between you and the information network.

But here's the problem: if high-quality information becomes one of the most valuable things on this network, we need better incentives to produce it.

Right now, the web's information economy is broken. Ad-supported models optimized for engagement, not quality. Paywalls that fragment access. No good way to attribute or compensate the long tail of knowledge creators.

In a world where AI clients are mediating access to information, we need new mechanisms. Direct agentic payments – where your AI can pay micropayments for quality information on your behalf. Quality signals that help distinguish good information from noise. Proper attribution so creators get credit and compensation.

This is one of the most important things we need to get right. Because without good incentives, we won't get good information. And without good information, these AI systems are useless.

// Loss of control over presentation

And here's what might be uncomfortable: you lose control over presentation.

Users aren't coming to your website anymore. Their AI agent queries your information and presents it however makes sense - possibly mixed with competitors, possibly in a format you never designed for. There's a new kind of responsive design here.

All you control is the information itself. Your craft shifts from pixel-perfect interfaces to information quality. The only things that matter: good information, well-structured, discoverable, trustworthy.

# What Does Being a Developer Mean? - Draft

So what does it mean to be a developer in this world?

Our job has never really been to write lots of software. It's been to help users. And that's still true - it's just that the nature of how we do that is shifting.

The skills that matter are changing. Understanding people, understanding the business, having taste - these become more valuable than knowing the latest React hooks. You succeed by deeply understanding what users need, not by mastering the framework du jour.

The technical skills that remain valuable are at the extremes. Lower-level systems work - building the AI platforms, the infrastructure. Or sophisticated professional software that needs human craft and precision. And then there's the human-centered work: information architecture, UX design of the critical paths you still control, brand, artistry.

But beyond these specific skills, there's something more fundamental we need to talk about. Because this entire conversation raises a question: what even is "the web"? And what's our responsibility as web developers in this transition?

# Software Distribution - Draft

This computer is very different. It is generating and composing interfaces & functionality together based on the user's intent and the task at hand.

For most things, functionality & views become more like composable pieces. Instead of downloading Uber and Spotify and your banking app and your airline app, all of that capability exists as discoverable, interoperable services that your system can compose together.

What we're really talking about is a fundamental decoupling – decoupling data from functionality, functionality from views, views from presentation, because we no longer need to *bake that functionality in* ahead of time, we can rely on a system backed by neural networks to do it on the fly. I think that takes us away from thinking about "apps" and more the lego pieces, and maybe some happy paths, for what the user might be trying to achieve.

// Tie to developers

So what does that mean for you as a developer? Well, if the system is composing most of the interface and orchestrating functionality, you're not spending your time building yet another CRUD app with React. Instead, you're thinking about: what are the capabilities I need to expose? What data structures make sense? What are the user journeys that matter for my business or my users?

You're designing the lego pieces, not assembling the whole castle. You're thinking about information architecture, about the quality of data you're providing, about the key experiences where you want control.

# What Does This Mean for Developers?

## Software distribution

- No longer distributing big monolithic apps (except specific cases like pro software)
- Might be fully synthesized by AI, or look like composable plugins/capabilities
- Discoverable, composable, interoperable functionality, decoupling of data, functionality, and views

## Information distribution

- High quality information becomes one of the most important part of the web: you can synthesize software, but not information
- This is actually a bit like the early web, the idea of a user agent (see Mediated Web talk)
- Need better incentives to produce better information ecosystem: direct agentic payments, quality signals, attribution

## What does it mean for developers?

- Our job isn't to write lots of software, it's to help users and help the ecosystem
- Skills shift: understanding people, the business, having taste > knowing React hooks
- What remains valuable: brand control, information architecture, UX design, artistry, lower-level OS work, sophisticated pro software
- What is "the web" really? It's about open networked computing
- Not just HTML/CSS/JS - it's an approach: openness, interoperability, open standards
- Need humans to advocate for & bring same principles to AI era
- Risk: proprietary walled gardens. Opportunity: ground level for building the next open computing paradigm.
- We did it once before, at the dawn of the web – let's do it again.
